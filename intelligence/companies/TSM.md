## Business summary

TSMC is the world's largest dedicated ("pure-play") semiconductor foundry,
manufacturing chips designed by fabless and integrated-device-manufacturer
customers rather than designing or selling its own branded chips [P1, P4].
As a foreign private issuer, TSMC reports primarily in New Taiwan Dollars and
files an annual Form 20-F and quarterly Form 6-K releases with the SEC.
TSMC manufactured 12,682 products for 534 customers in 2025 [P1].

First quarter 2026 revenue was NT$1,134.10 billion, with net income
attributable to shareholders of NT$572.48 billion, diluted EPS of NT$22.08,
gross margin of 66.2%, operating margin of 58.1%, and net margin of 50.5%
[P2]. Second quarter 2026 (ended June 30, 2026, reported July 16, 2026)
improved sequentially across every line: revenue NT$1,270.38 billion /
US$40.20 billion, up 36.0% year-over-year and at the high end of TSMC's
prior $39.0–$40.2 billion guided range; net income NT$706.56 billion, up
77.4% year-over-year; gross margin 67.7%, slightly above the prior
65.5%–67.5% guided range; operating margin 60.3%; net margin 55.6%; diluted
EPS NT$27.25 (US$4.31 per ADR) [P1, P2]. High-Performance Computing
represented 66% of Q2 2026 revenue, with Smartphone at 22%, IoT at 5%,
Automotive at 4%, and Digital Consumer Electronics at 1% [P2]. Advanced
process technologies (7-nanometer and more advanced) represented 77% of
wafer revenue, with node mix 2-nanometer 3%, 3-nanometer 30%, 5-nanometer
33%, and 7-nanometer 11% [P1].

At quarter-end, TSMC held NT$3,518.01 billion in cash and marketable
securities — a broader liquidity measure including short-term investments —
of which NT$3,134.22 billion was narrower cash and cash equivalents; these
are distinct figures and are kept separate throughout this record. Long-term
interest-bearing debt stood at NT$864.27 billion. Second-quarter operating
cash flow was NT$783.36 billion against capital expenditure of NT$496.00
billion, yielding free cash flow of NT$287.36 billion [P2]. TSMC's 2025 Form
20-F separately discloses aggregate long-term debt, including the current
portion, of NT$1,032.988 billion as of December 31, 2025 [P5] — a full-year
figure, not directly comparable to the quarter-end figure above without
further reconciliation.

Management raised full-year 2026 revenue-growth guidance to "slightly above
40%" in U.S. dollar terms and third-quarter 2026 guidance to revenue of
$44.6–45.8 billion, gross margin of 65–67%, and operating margin of 56–58%
[P2]. Management attributes the moderation from Q2's 67.7% actual gross
margin — to a Q3 guidance midpoint of 66%, only 1.7 percentage points lower —
partly to approximately 3 to 4 percentage points of second-half 2026
gross-margin dilution pressure from the ramp of the new 2-nanometer (N2)
node, substantially offset by strong leading-edge demand, cost improvement,
productivity gains, and across-node capacity optimization [P3]. N2 entered
high-volume manufacturing in the fourth quarter of 2025 with good yield,
while N2P and A16 are scheduled for second-half-2026 volume production [P4].

On the July 16, 2026 earnings call, TSMC announced an additional $100
billion investment in its U.S. operations; the company's own
investor-relations moderator, in the same call, stated that TSMC's total
planned Arizona investment is now $265 billion, and the Chief Executive
Officer's response to that question did not correct the figure [P3]. This
record uses $265 billion as TSMC's current total U.S. investment commitment
— a very large, newly expanded Arizona commitment.

TSMC's Form 20-F discloses that its largest customer represented 19% of 2025
revenue, its second-largest customer 17%, and its top ten customers combined
78% — **the filing does not name these customers**, and this record does not
rely on any secondary attribution of specific customer identities to reach
any conclusion below [P5].

TSMC paid a dividend of NT$18 per share for 2025; management stated on the
Q2 2026 call that shareholders will receive NT$24 per share in 2026, an
increase of approximately 33%, and stated an expectation of continuing,
increasing dividends in 2027 — **this 2027 statement is a management
expectation, not a declared or scheduled fact** [P3, P4].

## Eleven-question committee review

### 1. Economic/company role

TSM's role within the relevant economic system is manufacturing —
converting customer chip designs into working silicon — rather than chip
design itself [P1, P4]. This descriptive role is independent of, and not
derived from, `portfolio_role_ref`, which under this repository's frozen
doctrine (PI-0003) is a human-authored tier reference fixed at authoring
time. The role is not assumed to be a "bottleneck" from company size alone;
that question is addressed separately and more cautiously below.

### 2. Investment thesis

TSM is a leading-edge and advanced-packaging manufacturing capacity provider
capturing a disproportionate share of accelerating AI/HPC demand. The
evidence for this is an HPC platform at 66% of Q2 2026 revenue [P2],
financial results improving sequentially across every margin line from Q1
to Q2 2026 [P2, P1], and management's own direct attribution of its
simultaneously-raised FY2026 revenue-growth and capital-expenditure guidance
to AI, HPC, and agentic-AI demand [P3]. These are established facts. The
claim that this demand pattern is durable, multi-year secular growth — as
opposed to an elevated but ultimately cyclical peak — is management's own
characterization [P3], not independently provable from the facts alone, and
this record does not treat it as settled. The thesis would be falsified by
any of the conditions listed under Thesis-break conditions below.

### 3. Moat

TSM's competitive advantage combines demonstrated technology leadership (77%
of Q2 2026 wafer revenue at 7-nanometer-and-below, with N2 in high-volume
manufacturing since Q4 2025 with good yield and N2P/A16 both scheduled for
second-half 2026 volume production) [P1, P4], genuine and specifically
characterized switching costs (management has described selecting and
ramping a new foundry-technology partner as requiring roughly five years —
encompassing technology evaluation, test-chip development, and joint
capacity preparation) [P3], manufacturing scale (approximately 72%
pure-play foundry market share in Q1 2026, per TrendForce) [P7], and tight
advanced-packaging capacity, which management has stated is constraining
customer growth [P3]. What could weaken this moat: successful, at-scale
execution by a competing foundry at the leading edge; a major customer's
successful qualification of an alternative supplier; or geopolitical
disruption forcing customer diversification regardless of technical merit.
The evidence supports current, demonstrated superiority — it does not
establish that this superiority is permanent.

### 4. Bottleneck power

**Strong leading-edge and advanced-packaging position with high switching
costs and conditional bottleneck characteristics; the evidence does not
establish permanent or irreplaceable sole-source status.** This conclusion
rests on the technology-mix, switching-cost, packaging-tightness, and
market-share evidence cited under Moat above. It explicitly does not rest
on, and this record does not assert, that TSM is "effectively sole-source,"
that its bottleneck position is permanent, that TSM holds any specific
share of the most-advanced nodes beyond the 72% figure cited above [P7], or
that any specific competitor yield gap has been established.

### 5. Financial quality

TSM's Q2 2026 gross, operating, and net margins (67.7%/60.3%/55.6%)
improved from Q1 2026 (66.2%/58.1%/50.5%) [P2, P1]. The balance sheet shows
NT$3,134.22 billion in ending cash against NT$864.27 billion in long-term
interest-bearing debt, a materially net-cash position by simple subtraction
[P2]. Free cash flow was positive at NT$287.36 billion in the second quarter
alone — NT$783.36 billion in operating cash flow less NT$496.00 billion in
capital expenditure — with capital expenditure representing approximately
39% of the quarter's revenue on a simple division [P2]. TSMC's 2025 Form
20-F separately discloses aggregate long-term debt, including the current
portion, of NT$1,032.988 billion as of December 31, 2025 [P5]. This record
does not have evidence of how TSM's financial profile would behave in a
prior semiconductor-cycle downturn — that remains an open, unaddressed
question in the sources reviewed.

### 6. Capital allocation

The overwhelming majority of TSM's capital is directed to capital
expenditure — NT$496.00 billion in the second quarter of 2026 alone, amid a
full-year 2026 guidance range of $60–64 billion [P2, P3] — including a very
large, newly expanded $265 billion total commitment to Arizona manufacturing
[P3]. TSM also pays a growing dividend: NT$18 per share for 2025, an
expected NT$24 per share for 2026, and a management expectation (not a
scheduled fact) of continued increases in 2027 [P3, P4]. No formal
share-buyback program was identified in the sources reviewed. Positive free
cash flow (NT$287.36 billion in the second quarter) despite this capital
intensity is a fact-based, non-price-dependent signal that the core
business's cash generation is outrunning its reinvestment program, at least
in the period observed.

### 7. Secular tailwinds

High-Performance Computing represented 66% of Q2 2026 revenue [P2], and the
2025 annual report discusses robust AI-related demand as a driver of the
business [P4]. Smartphone (22%), Automotive (4%), IoT (5%), and Digital
Consumer Electronics (1%) round out the disclosed platform mix [P2].
Whether the AI/HPC-driven strength represents durable secular demand or an
elevated point in an ordinary semiconductor cycle is not resolved by the
evidence available to this record.

### 8. Execution risk

Already established: N2 entered high-volume manufacturing in the fourth
quarter of 2025 with good yield [P4]. Still dependent on future execution:
N2P and A16 ramping successfully to their scheduled second-half-2026
volume-production dates [P4]; the net Q3 2026 margin outcome landing near
the guided 66% midpoint as the N2 dilution and its offsetting factors play
out in practice [P2, P3]; successful ramp of the Arizona, Japan, and Germany
overseas facilities at the scale now committed [P3, P4]; continued expansion
of advanced-packaging capacity against management's own stated current
tightness [P3]; and sustained delivery of the "slightly above 40%" full-year
2026 revenue-growth guidance itself, which remains guidance, not yet a
completed fact.

### 9. Portfolio uniqueness

*(Research analysis only — this section does not recommend any allocation
action.)* TSM provides direct, dedicated foundry and advanced-manufacturing
exposure. Portfolio-HQ currently also holds INTC, so TSM is not the
portfolio's only holding with some semiconductor-manufacturing or
foundry-related exposure. This packet does not establish the degree to
which INTC's economic exposure overlaps with TSM's dedicated-foundry
business. TSM's distinctiveness should therefore be described as more
direct foundry exposure, not as categorically unique manufacturing exposure.
The degree of overlap remains unresolved.

### 10. Replacement candidate

*(Descriptive comparison only — not a recommendation to replace, add, trim,
buy, or sell anything.)* This packet does not establish an operational
replacement for TSMC. Samsung Foundry and Intel Foundry were considered as
possible comparative subjects, but the approved P1–P7 source set does not
establish that either is interchangeable with TSMC for a particular
customer, node, product, yield requirement, capacity requirement, or
qualification timeline. INTC is already a current Portfolio-HQ holding, but
this fact does not establish that it provides equivalent portfolio exposure
or operational substitutability. Replacement and substitutability remain
unresolved.

### 11. Capital Priority

*(Using only ONTO-0001's bounded definition: the human-reviewed case for
whether additional capital deserves consideration relative to current
policy and available alternatives — not a score, rank, league table,
next-dollar list, buy recommendation, target weight, tier change,
promotion, or demotion.)* Evidence supporting consideration of additional
capital includes sequentially-improving financial quality, a materially
net-cash balance sheet with strong free cash flow despite record capital
intensity, officially-disclosed HPC demand strength combined with
management's own direct AI-demand attribution, and a concrete, growing
dividend. Evidence arguing for caution includes a precisely-characterized
and dated regulatory risk (the Nanjing export license's uncertain renewal),
extreme and quantified capital intensity, officially-disclosed but unnamed
customer concentration, the scale and execution risk of the newly-confirmed
$265 billion Arizona commitment, and the still-unresolved question of
whether current AI/HPC demand is durable or cyclically elevated. Current
portfolio policy (T1 tier, 3.35% target, membership in the `semis`
correlated-cluster cap) is reported here as context only, not as an input
to this discussion. No score, ranking, or next-dollar conclusion is offered.

## Risks — detail

**Customer concentration.** Largest customer 19%, second-largest 17%, top
ten combined 78% of 2025 revenue — the filing does not name these customers
[P5]. Mitigating evidence: a broad remaining customer base (534 customers as
of 2025, per P1) and platform diversification. Classification: structural,
officially disclosed.

**Taiwan/cross-strait geopolitical and political-stability exposure.**
TSMC's Form 20-F discloses geopolitical and political-stability exposure,
and tariff/sanctions/export-control risk more broadly, as risk-factor
categories, distinct from the Nanjing-specific license risk below [P5].
Mitigating evidence: overseas-fab diversification (Arizona, Japan,
Germany), though still capacity-limited and ramping. Classification:
structural.

**Nanjing (China) facility export-license risk.** The VEU authorization for
TSMC's Nanjing facility expired in December 2025; an annual export license
currently permits continued operation; renewal or continuation is not
assured [P5]. This is among the most concretely time-bound and specifically
sourced risks in this record. Mitigating evidence: the license is currently
valid and operations are continuing under it. Classification: structural,
currently active, directly filed.

**Taiwan natural-disaster and utility-disruption exposure.** TSMC's Form
20-F discloses risk from earthquakes, typhoons, floods, droughts, and
water/electricity/utility disruption, and specifically states recognized,
insurance-net losses of approximately NT$3.0 billion from an April 2024
earthquake event and approximately NT$5.3 billion from a January 2025
earthquake event [P5]. Mitigating evidence: both losses were partially
offset by insurance, and no source reviewed reports a structural-damage-
driven production halt from either event. Classification: current,
realized, and recurring within a roughly nine-month window.

**Customer-curtailment, sourcing-policy, and outsourcing-policy changes.**
Disclosed as its own risk-factor category [P5]. Mitigating evidence: the
depth of design-in relationships and the roughly five-year switching-cost
characterization discussed under Moat. Classification: structural.

**Competition.** Disclosed as a risk-factor category [P5]; TrendForce
places TSMC's Q1 2026 pure-play foundry share at 72% [P7]. Mitigating
evidence: TSM's current 77% advanced-technology revenue mix. Classification:
emerging.

**Semiconductor cyclicality.** Disclosed as a risk-factor category [P5].
Mitigating evidence: current AI/HPC demand is running counter to any
broader cyclical softness, based on the results and guidance above.
Classification: emerging/uncertain.

**Technology-leadership risk.** Disclosed as a risk-factor category [P5].
Mitigating evidence: the current 77% advanced-technology mix and the
second-half-2026 N2P/A16 roadmap. Classification: structural, actively
managed.

**Raw-material and equipment-supply constraints.** Disclosed as a
risk-factor category [P5]. No specific current shortage identified in the
sources reviewed. Classification: structural.

**N2/node-transition execution.** Approximately 3–4 percentage points of
second-half-2026 gross-margin dilution pressure, substantially offset by
strong leading-edge demand, cost improvement, productivity gains, and
across-node capacity optimization, with the net effect reflected in a Q3
2026 gross-margin guidance midpoint of 66% — only 1.7 percentage points
below Q2's actual 67.7% [P2, P3]. Classification: current, actively guided.

**Overseas-fab ramp and cost execution.** A very large, newly expanded
Arizona investment commitment (US$265 billion total) plus Japan and Germany
facilities carry multi-year construction, staffing, and cost-to-plan
execution risk [P3, P4]. Classification: current/structural.

**Extreme capital intensity.** Second-quarter 2026 capital expenditure of
NT$496.00 billion represented approximately 39% of that quarter's revenue,
amid a full-year 2026 guidance range of $60–64 billion [P2, P3]. Mitigating
evidence: free cash flow remained positive (NT$287.36 billion) even at this
capex level. Classification: structural.

**Technological displacement.** No source reviewed for this record
addresses an imminent threat to silicon-based semiconductor manufacturing
as the dominant compute paradigm. Recorded honestly as insufficient
evidence rather than asserted or dismissed. Classification: insufficient
evidence.

## Thesis-break conditions

1. A sustained, multi-quarter HPC-revenue deceleration or reversal not
   explained by ordinary seasonality.
2. A material, disclosed reduction in orders from either officially-
   disclosed top-two customer (19%/17% concentration).
3. Evidence that advanced-packaging capacity additions outrun real demand,
   reversing management's own stated tightness.
4. A credible, at-scale qualification of a major customer's next-generation
   product on a competing foundry's leading-edge node.
5. A material, disclosed escalation in Taiwan/cross-strait conditions, or
   non-renewal of the Nanjing annual export license.
6. Gross or operating margin falling materially below TSM's own guided
   ranges for two or more consecutive quarters without a clearly identified
   one-time cause.
7. A disclosed material overseas-fab cost overrun or schedule slip beyond
   guided ranges, or an officially-confirmed N2P/A16 slip beyond second-half
   2026 from a source directly superseding the 2025 annual report.
