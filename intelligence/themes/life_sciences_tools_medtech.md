## Description

Companies whose disclosed core business is either (a) medical and
surgical devices used directly in clinical patient care, or (b)
life-sciences research tools, diagnostics instrumentation, reagents,
and consumables used in laboratory, biopharma, and diagnostic
settings. These are not the same business:

- Group (a) sells to hospitals and surgeons and is paid per-procedure
  or per-system, with reimbursement and hospital capital-budget
  exposure.
- Group (b) sells to labs, biopharma companies, and diagnostic
  operators, and is paid via instrument capital sales plus recurring
  consumables, reagents, and service revenue, with biopharma- and
  lab-funding-cycle exposure instead.

The common economic connection is narrower than "healthcare": both
groups monetize a shared underlying dynamic -- rising global demand
for advanced clinical procedures and for the tools and consumables
that life-sciences R&D and diagnostics consume -- but the theme
explicitly does not claim these are the same customers, the same
revenue model, the same risk profile, or the same cycle. They are two
distinct sub-industries linked by a shared secular driver, not one
industry described two ways.

## Evidence

- ISRG (Intuitive Surgical) reported FY2025 revenue of $10.1 billion,
  up 21% year-over-year, with worldwide procedures (da Vinci and Ion
  combined) up approximately 19% (da Vinci procedures up approximately
  18%, Ion procedures up approximately 51%). Systems revenue was $2.47
  billion (+26%); Instruments and accessories revenue was $6.02
  billion (+19%); Service revenue was $1.57 billion (+20%). The da
  Vinci installed base reached 11,106 systems at 2025 year-end, up 12%
  from 9,902 at the end of 2024. In the fourth quarter of 2025, 532 da
  Vinci systems were placed, of which 303 were da Vinci 5 systems.
- TMO (Thermo Fisher Scientific) reported FY2025 total revenue of
  $44.56 billion, up 4%. Reported segment revenue: Life Sciences
  Solutions $10.37 billion (+8%); Analytical Instruments $7.55 billion
  (+1%); Specialty Diagnostics $4.68 billion (+4%); Laboratory
  Products and Biopharma Services $23.98 billion (+4%). **Laboratory
  Products and Biopharma Services is the largest reported segment by
  segment revenue.** The four segment totals sum to approximately
  $46.58 billion, which is **not directly additive** to the $44.56
  billion consolidated total -- the approximately $2.0 billion
  difference was not reconciled against a directly inspected
  eliminations table, and no conclusion in this record relies on
  summing the four segment figures.
- SYK (Stryker) reported FY2025 net sales of $25.116 billion, up
  11.2%. MedSurg and Neurotechnology segment revenue was $15.647
  billion, up 15.7% as reported. Orthopaedics segment revenue was
  $9.469 billion, up 4.3% as reported, up 3.8% on a constant-currency
  basis, and up 9.5% organically (excluding a -5.7% impact from
  acquisitions and divestitures). Stryker's own disclosure describes a
  new "Ortho Tech" business, effective Q1 2026, combining the
  orthopaedic instruments portfolio with the Mako and enabling-
  technologies portfolio, with 2023-2025 financial statements recast
  to align with the new structure. **SYK is not a member of this
  theme** -- deferred, not rejected (see Deferred Companies below).
  Two figures found in earlier research -- 16.5% for MedSurg and
  Neurotechnology growth, and 5.4% for Orthopaedics growth -- could
  not be traced to a specific, identifiable basis and are withdrawn;
  they must not appear as live factual claims anywhere in this record
  or in any future SYK record.
- DHR (Danaher) reported FY2025 total revenue of $24.6 billion, up
  3.0% as reported and up 2.0% on a non-GAAP core-revenue basis. The
  Biotechnology segment's core revenue was up 7%. The Life Sciences
  segment's core revenue was down 4%. The Diagnostics segment's core
  revenue was down 1.5%. Danaher's current structure comprises three
  reportable segments -- Biotechnology, Life Sciences, and Diagnostics
  -- following the 2023 separation of the Veralto business. **DHR is
  not a member of this theme** -- deferred, not rejected (see Deferred
  Companies below).

**Interpretation, separated from the facts above**: these figures are
consistent with a real, evidenced theme spanning two distinct
sub-industries. This is an observation about the pattern across
independently corroborated figures for the two approved members
(ISRG, TMO) -- it is not a forecast that current growth rates
continue, and it is not a valuation or allocation judgment.

## Risks

- Regulatory and product-approval risk applies to device companies
  bringing new surgical systems and implants to market, and separately
  to tools/diagnostics companies via instrument and diagnostic-assay
  clearance pathways -- these are not the same regulatory process.
- Reimbursement and hospital-capital-spending exposure applies to
  device companies specifically; it does not meaningfully apply to
  tools/diagnostics companies' lab and biopharma customer base in the
  same way.
- Procedure-volume sensitivity applies to device companies; it is not
  a meaningful driver for tools/diagnostics companies.
- Research, pharmaceutical, biotech, and laboratory spending-cycle
  sensitivity applies directly to tools/diagnostics companies; it does
  not meaningfully apply to device companies.
- China and other geographic exposure is a plausible risk category for
  multinational companies in this theme; not independently quantified
  with company-specific figures in this record.
- Foreign-exchange effects are a disclosed factor reflected in the gap
  between reported and organic/constant-currency growth rates.
- Execution risk in product launches and innovation pipelines applies
  across the theme -- an active platform rollout for a device company,
  or a disclosed structural reorganization for a company under review.
- Supply-chain or manufacturing concentration is a plausible risk
  category generally; not independently confirmed with company-
  specific detail in this record.

Not every risk above applies to every company. The device-side risks
and the tools-side risks are largely non-overlapping, which is itself
evidence the theme correctly captures two distinct businesses rather
than one.

## Catalysts

- Continued procedure growth and installed-base expansion, as
  disclosed by device companies each quarter.
- Continued platform adoption pace for newer device generations.
- Continued growth in recurring consumables, reagents, and services
  revenue at tools/diagnostics companies.
- Recovery, or continued softness, in biopharma and laboratory
  end-market demand, as disclosed each quarter.

No specific future date is asserted for any catalyst above.

## Non-membership

Membership requires that the company's material disclosed revenue
come directly from selling medical or surgical devices for clinical
care, or from selling life-sciences research tools, diagnostics
instrumentation, reagents, or consumables. This explicitly excludes:

- Pharmaceutical or biotechnology companies whose revenue is
  therapeutics or drugs rather than tools or devices
- Managed care or health-insurance companies
- Healthcare-delivery or services companies such as hospital systems
  or clinics
- General industrial manufacturers with incidental healthcare exposure
  but no material disclosed healthcare-specific segment
- Software companies that merely serve healthcare customers without
  deriving material disclosed revenue from selling medical or
  laboratory tools or devices themselves
- Companies that use medical or laboratory technology internally --
  such as a hospital operator or a contract research organization --
  but do not derive material disclosed revenue from selling it

## Deferred companies

**SYK (Stryker) -- deferred, not rejected.** Stryker's disclosed
FY2025 results are strong, and its reconciled segment figures (see
Evidence above) present a solid underlying picture. Deferral is based
on Stryker's disclosed, in-progress structural reorganization -- a new
"Ortho Tech" business combining the orthopaedic instruments portfolio
with the Mako and enabling-technologies portfolio, effective Q1 2026,
with 2023-2025 financial statements already recast once. Writing a
segment-level thesis and risk record now, before this transition has
been observable across multiple reporting periods under the new
structure, risks producing a record that needs early revision.

**DHR (Danaher) -- deferred, not rejected.** Danaher's Biotechnology
segment shows real, disclosed strength, but this sits alongside
disclosed core-revenue declines in two of Danaher's three segments --
Life Sciences and Diagnostics -- for a currently mixed, not uniformly
positive, overall picture. This is not disqualifying on its own, but
it argues against using one of only two initial pilot slots on the
candidate with the most unresolved disclosed softness when a cleaner,
more consistently positive alternative (TMO) is available to represent
the same tools/diagnostics sub-industry.

## Lifecycle

**Established.**

Rationale: multiple independent companies (ISRG and TMO, in the
approved pilot scope) span two genuinely distinct sub-industries.
Demonstrated commercial deployment is present -- every figure cited
above is a realized, reported result, not a projection. Recurring
disclosure across reporting periods is present -- both companies have
a multi-decade continuous public reporting history in these business
lines, making this a mature industry grouping rather than a nascent
one. Not Emerging: neither business category is new or unproven. Not
Mature or Declining: disclosed growth remains real and positive at
both approved members. Lifecycle is not inferred from stock
performance, valuation, or recent popularity.
