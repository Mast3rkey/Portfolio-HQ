# Margin Doctrine

_2026-07-17 · The constitutional document for margin in this system. Every margin-related design, backtest, or code change is checked against this page before it's built. If a future proposal doesn't fit here, the proposal changes — not this page, and not without a new Decisions Log entry explaining why the doctrine itself is being revised._

---

## The one-sentence version

**Margin only amplifies whatever edge the underlying portfolio already has. It is not itself a source of edge, and its job in this system is risk governance, not return generation.**

## What margin is

Margin is borrowed buying power collateralized against the whole account. It lets the portfolio hold more gross exposure than net equity alone would allow. That's the entire mechanism — there is no sense in which margin *picks* better positions, *times* the market better, or *knows* something the unlevered portfolio didn't already know. Every dollar of return margin produces is a multiple of a return the portfolio was already going to earn (or lose) on its own. Every dollar of loss margin produces is the same multiple, in the same direction, on the way down.

## What margin is not

- **Not an alpha source.** It cannot turn a mediocre portfolio into a good one. If the underlying holdings don't have edge, margin just loses money faster, with interest added on top.
- **Not a discretionary timing tool.** Discretionary or predictive judgment may never increase leverage beyond a governed recommendation or the fixed cap, and may never bypass the buffer floor or a repayment rule — this remains absolute. The operator may always decline a recommendation, use less margin than recommended or allowed, or repay more or sooner than any rule requires: discretion here is asymmetric, always available to reduce risk, never available to exceed a governed limit. Separately, whether a rules-based, pre-registered, evidence-gated conditional rule — keyed to observable account- or market-state conditions — can produce a superior net risk-adjusted outcome than this fixed posture is an open empirical question, researchable only under its own separately governed research charter and adoptable only through its own separate governance decision (`governance/decisions/GOV-0003-margin-conditional-research-permitted.md`). Opening that research authorizes nothing on its own.
- **Not free.** Margin interest (~5% APR, first $1,000 free, per the Robinhood terms this account uses) is a guaranteed, certain cost — a hurdle rate the levered portion of the portfolio must clear just to break even, before any consideration of the extra risk taken on. It is never treated as a rounding error.
- **Not a discretionary lever.** Every existing margin rule in this system (the 1.8x leverage cap, the 30% buffer floor) is a fixed, mechanical ceiling/floor with no exception path — the same posture as the crypto sleeve's fixed conviction-sizing and the cluster caps' fixed concentration limits. Margin Intelligence extends this posture; it does not introduce a new one.

## The system's job, precisely

1. **Measure whether leverage has historically helped**, honestly, on a risk-adjusted basis, before assuming it should continue. This was tested: Phase 3–7A's margin-drag-vs-unlevered comparison (`docs/PHASE7_RESEARCH_PROGRAM_CLOSURE.md`) found a 1.8x-levered account beat an unlevered one on TWR (+5.58pp) and lost on MaxDD (-4.40pp) across the 2021–2026 window — a hypothetical, simulated result, not a claim about this account's own real history. That result did **not** prove 1.8x optimal, and did not prove any other level safe: it is one honest data point in one window, evaluated once under the program's own pre-committed discipline, not a search for a better number. The current 1.8x cap remains set at "the level in place when the debt was discovered" (2026-07-13) — a doctrine choice, not an evidence-derived one; this test measured whether carrying margin at all clears a risk-adjusted bar, it did not optimize the level. Conditional or timing-based deployment (adjusting *when* or *whether* to draw margin based on account- or market-state) was explicitly outside Phase 3–7A's charter, not an oversight later discovered — see `governance/decisions/MARGIN-0004-phase3-7a-closure-reconciliation.md` for the full reconciliation of what this program tested and did not test, and `governance/decisions/GOV-0003-margin-conditional-research-permitted.md` for the separately governed research question that remains open.
2. **Prevent leverage from exceeding survivable limits.** "Survivable" means: the account can hold through a realistic historical stress period without a forced, capital-destroying liquidation — not "survivable" in the sense of maximizing return across some backtested window. This is a materially different optimization target than return-maximization, and every leverage-level test this system runs must be evaluated against survivability first, return second.
3. **Identify when repayment improves risk-adjusted outcomes**, using the same evidence-based standard as everything else — a repayment rule earns its way into production by beating a clearly stated baseline on a pre-committed threshold, exactly like every trim rule, cap, and gate this system has ever adopted.

## What "risk governance" means operationally

- Margin availability is a **ceiling that responds to the account's own state** (leverage ratio, buffer %, concentration) — never to the market's state.
- Margin state (Normal / Elevated / Forced Deleveraging, per the Margin Intelligence Engine design) exists to **describe risk that already exists**, not to **forecast risk that might arrive.**
- Repayment logic exists to **reduce standing risk when it's elevated**, using cash flows that are already happening for other, already-approved reasons (trims) — never to **react to a market forecast.**
- Every number this system produces about margin must be explainable in one sentence without reference to where the market is headed. "Leverage is at 74% of the cap" is explainable. "We're paying down debt because a correction looks likely" is not, and will never appear in this system's output.

A future, separately governed, evidence-gated conditional rule (see `governance/decisions/GOV-0003-margin-conditional-research-permitted.md`) may one day change what "the account's own state" is permitted to include as an input — but only after its own approval; until then, every line above stands exactly as written.

## Margin Intelligence Engine Doctrine

The section above establishes what margin *is*. This section establishes, explicitly and by name, what the Margin Intelligence Engine — the system being designed in `docs/MARGIN_INTELLIGENCE_DESIGN.md` — is and is not allowed to answer. **Margin is not a signal generator.** Every question below was chosen because it's either a real risk-governance question this engine should eventually answer, or a real timing question dressed up as a risk question that it must not.

**It can answer:**
- ✅ **How much leverage can the portfolio safely carry?** — a survivability question, answered from the portfolio's own structure (concentration, historical stress behavior) and the fixed leverage cap, never from a market read.
- ✅ **When should debt reduction become a priority?** — a question about the account's own state (leverage ratio relative to the cap, buffer relative to the floor), answered by the margin-state classifier (`MARGIN_INTELLIGENCE_DESIGN.md` §2), never by a forecast.
- ✅ **How much risk capacity remains?** — the utilization and concentration-adjusted headroom formulas (`MARGIN_INTELLIGENCE_DESIGN.md` §3) are pure arithmetic on the account's current numbers, nothing else.
- ✅ **What happens under stress scenarios?** — the single-name/single-position decomposition already proven in `t1t2_trim_backtest.md` (NVDA's 2.14x/-66.4% analysis), and the leverage-sweep backtest proposed in `MARGIN_INTELLIGENCE_DESIGN.md` §5, both ask "how bad could this get," never "is now a bad time."

**It cannot answer:**
- ❌ **"Should we borrow because NVDA looks cheap?"** — a valuation judgment. Excluded system-wide by the Guardrails' no-predictive-research rule, and specifically excluded from margin by the 2026-07-13 Decisions Log entry.
- ❌ **"Should we increase leverage because volatility is low?"** — volatility may be used as an input to a risk-*sizing* calculation (translating a hypothetical drawdown into an equity-impact estimate) but never as a trigger that moves the leverage ceiling itself. Low volatility is not evidence that borrowing more is safe; it's frequently the precondition for the next drawdown, per the doctrine's own standing warning about pro-cyclical lever-up. Whether a pre-registered, evidence-gated rule keyed to volatility could be *researched* — not adopted — is addressed separately under `GOV-0003`'s permitted research scope; this engine computes no such trigger today.
- ❌ **"Should we time margin around drawdowns?"** — this is averaging down with borrowed money, under a different name. Not a backtest question (nothing to test — the answer is structurally no, per the doctrine's core identity), and not in scope for any phase of this engine, present or future. The same `GOV-0003` research-scope caveat applies: researchable under its own charter, not adopted, not operational.

**Why this list exists as its own named section, not just implied by the rest of the page:** every prior review pass in this project (the Portfolio HQ audit, the Margin Intelligence design revisions, both research tracks) has independently re-derived some version of this ✅/❌ boundary from first principles. Naming it once, explicitly, here, means a future proposal can be checked against this list directly instead of requiring the boundary to be re-argued from the doctrine's prose every time. This keeps the system aligned with the Decisions Log by construction, not by re-litigation.

## How this doctrine gets tested, and how it can change

This page is not exempt from the same evidence standard it imposes on everything else — but the standard for *testing this page* is different from the standard for testing a specific rule. The core claim ("margin is not itself a source of edge") is a mathematical identity, not an empirical claim — leverage is definitionally a multiplier on existing returns, so no backtest is needed or possible to "prove" it, the same way no backtest is needed to prove that trimming a position reduces its dollar exposure. What *is* empirical, and what Phase 1 exists to test, is narrower and different: **given that identity, does carrying margin at this account's actual cost of capital, at some specific leverage level, produce a better risk-adjusted outcome than not carrying it — net of interest, net of the extra drawdown risk.** That's a real question with a real answer this system doesn't yet have. This doctrine page will be updated with a dated entry once Phase 1 produces that answer, the same way every other closed backtest question gets logged in `CLAUDE.md`'s Decisions Log — not before.

**Dated closure (2026-07-21, `MARGIN-0004`):** Phase 3–7A produced the answer this section anticipated — see "The system's job, precisely" bullet 1 above for the specific result (hypothetical, simulated: +5.58pp TWR / -4.40pp MaxDD, 1.8x vs. unlevered). `docs/PHASE5B_GOVERNANCE_DECISION.md` evaluated that evidence, alongside every other Phase 3–7A finding, and decided: maintain the then-current production posture — no repayment logic, no leverage-level change, margin stays research-only. `governance/decisions/GOV-0003-margin-conditional-research-permitted.md` (2026-07-21) later narrowed this doctrine's absolute timing prohibition to open a distinct, narrower question — whether a rules-based, pre-registered, evidence-gated conditional deployment or repayment rule can outperform this fixed posture — researchable only under its own separate charter, adoptable only through its own separate governance decision. **Recording this history authorizes no new rule, no charter, no code, and no production change** — see `governance/decisions/MARGIN-0004-phase3-7a-closure-reconciliation.md` for the full reconciliation.
